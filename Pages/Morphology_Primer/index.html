Morphological Features of Amanita

This guide explains the key morphological features used to identify Amanita species. Each feature is organized by the part of the mushroom: the cap (pileus), the cup-like base (volva), and the stem (stipe). Expand each section below to learn more about what to look for.

Amanita mushroom development

The seven stages of Amanita mushroom development are shown below. The stages are not always distinct and can vary in duration depending on environmental conditions.

Cap (Pileus)

Diameter

Definition

The width of the cap measured across the widest point.

Variations

Due to mushroom size plasticity, sorting species into distinct size categories can sometimes help organize them.

  • Small: Less than 40mm (~1.5 in)
  • Medium: 40-80mm (~1.5-3 in)
  • Large: Greater than 80mm (~3+ in)

Important note:

Size can vary drastically within a species depending on conditions and maturity. Noting relative maturity stage can help alleviate confusion.

Photo: Cap Diameter Measurement

Amanita solaniolens — specimen 30235

Center Color

Common Colors

  • White: Pure white, but often found with subtle yellow or even pink hues in the center
  • Yellow/Green: Pale to bright, citrine yellows or greenish tones
  • Yellow/Orange: Bright yellow to deep orange right at the center
  • Brown/Red: Ranging from light tan to deep brown sometimes with reddish staining
  • Gray: Silvery or muted gray tones. Some species exhibit both gray and brown mushrooms

What to Look For

Observe the cap color in natural light if possible. Staining or bruising may change color over time so document color before removing mushroom

Margin Color

Definition

The color of the edge or outer rim of the cap. Often differs from the center even just slightly.

Photo: Cap Margin Colors

Amanita porphyria — specimen 30207

Amanita aff. fulva — specimen 30243

Shape

Descriptive terms

  • Convex: Rounded like an upside-down bowl
  • Plano-convex: Flat center with rounded edges
  • Applanate/planar: Flat
  • Campanulate: Bell-shaped
  • Plano-concave: Flat to slightly depressed in center

What to Look For

View the cap from the side. Note that young mushrooms often have more convex caps that flatten or depress as they age.

Photo: Cap Shape Examples

Amanita rhacopus — specimen 30233 planar

Amanita frostiana — specimen 30229 convex

Amanita jakeslandingensis nom. prov. — specimen 30268 plano-concave

Surface Texture

Definition

The feel and appearance of the cap surface: whether it is smooth, wrinkled, viscid, or has other surface characteristics.

Texture Types

  • Glabrous: Smooth to the touch, skin-like
  • Dry: Matte appearance and feel
  • Viscid: Slimy or slippery when wet, sticky when moist
  • Silky: Shiny and smooth with a silken appearance
  • Wrinkly: Often due to drying or age

What to Look For

Feel the cap gently and observe in different lighting. Viscid caps may lose this quality as they dry. Check if there are visible striations or wrinkles.

Amanita multisquamosa — specimen 30222 silky

Amanita multisquamosa — specimen 30223 viscid

Amanita amerivirosa — specimen 30195 Wrinkly

Umbonate

Definition

The presence or absence of a small, rounded bump or knob at the center (apex) of the cap.

Conditions

  • Umbonate: Clear central bump present
  • Not umbonate (Rounded/Flat): Center is rounded or flat without a distinct bump

What to Look For

Look at the cap from the side and top. A small central bump should be visible if present. This feature can diminish as the mushroom matures.

Amanita cf. aurorae nom prov. — specimen 30185 umbonate

Amanita jakeslandingensis nom. prov. — specimen 30269 umbonate

Amanita rhacopus — specimen 30189 minutely umbonate

Striation / Grooves

Definition

Fine radial lines or grooves running from the center toward the margin, often visible near the edge. Measured in millimeters from the margin inward.

Striation Patterns

  • Striate: Visible lines but no texture
  • Sulcate: Textured lines

Amanita populiphila — specimen 30280

Amanita subnigra nom. prov. — specimen 30262

Appendiculate / Veil Fragments on Cap

Definition

The presence of fragments of the partial veil hanging from the cap margin, appearing as strands or tufts on the edge.

What to Look For

Check the margin of the cap for any hanging veil material, especially in young mushrooms. These fragments often wear away as the mushroom ages or weathers.

Photo: Cap Margin Fragments

Amanita cf. lavendula — specimen 30219

Universal Veil Presence (Warts/Patches)

Definition

Remnants of the universal veil covering young mushrooms, appearing as warts, patches, or pulverized residue on the cap surface.

Veil Conditions

  • Warts: Scattered bumpy pieces
  • Patches: Larger, flatter pieces of veil material
  • Powder (pulverulent): Fine, pulverized veil material
  • Scales: Odd shaped, scab-like material that are hard to remove

What to Look For

Look for raised bumps or white/pale patches on the colored cap surface. These are more visible on young specimens. They can wash off in rain or wear away with handling.

Amanita multisquamosa — specimen 30222 Small warts

Amanita fusca — specimen 30281 Large patch

Amanita porphyria — specimen 30286 Powder (pulverulent)

Staining

Definition

Discoloration or color changes on the cap resulting from bruising, handling, or oxidation of damaged tissue.

Staining Types

  • No staining: Cap color remains uniform
  • Brown staining: Bruised areas turn brown
  • Red/Pink staining: Bruised areas turn red or pinkish

What to Look For

Observe if the cap has discolored areas from recent bruising. This is most obvious on young mushrooms. Handle gently to avoid creating additional staining.

Amanita amerirubescens nom. prov. — specimen 30232 red staining

Stipe (Stem)

Length

Definition

The height of the stipe from the cap attachment to the bottom of basal bulb if present, measured in millimeters. If there is no bulb, measure to the base.

Typical Lengths

  • Short: Less than 50mm (2 in)
  • Medium: 50-120mm (2-5 in)
  • Tall: Greater than 120mm (5+ in)

Amanita fusca — specimen 30186

Amanita suballiacea — specimen 30187

Width / Thickness

Definition

The diameter of the stipe, typically measured in millimeters, can be consistent along the length or taper.

Width Ranges

  • Thin: Less than 5mm diameter
  • Medium: 5-15mm diameter
  • Thick: Greater than 15mm diameter

What to Look For

Measure the diameter of the stipe mid-way along its length. Note if the stipe is uniform, tapers toward the top, or widens at the base.

Amanita cf. aurorae nom prov. — specimen 30185

Amanita populiphila — specimen 30280

Color

Definition

The color of the stipe surface, which may be uniform, gradated, or include zones of different colors.

Common Stipe Colors

  • White: Most common; pure or off-white
  • Pale yellow/Cream: Light colored
  • Yellow/Green: Distinct yellow or greenish tones
  • Gray/Brown: Darker or muted tones

What to Look For

Observe the stipe color in natural light. Note if the color is uniform from top to bottom or if it changes (e.g., yellower at the base).

Amanita suballiacea — specimen 30182

Amanita amerirubescens nom prov. — specimen 30230

Amanita cf. lavendula — specimen 30218

Shape

Definition

The profile of the stipe along its length: whether it is uniform, tapered, bulbous, or curved.

Stipe Shapes

  • Cylindrical: Uniform diameter throughout
  • Tapered: Narrows gradually toward the apex (top)
  • Bulbous: Widened base, narrower above
  • Curved: Bent or arched along its length

What to Look For

Observe the stipe from the side. Note if it maintains consistent width or if there are bulges, tapers, or curves.

Amanita brunnescens — specimen 30259

Decoration / Surface Features

Definition

Surface patterns on the stipe including zones, grooves, scales, or fibrous patterns. Noted separately for the upper and lower portions.

Decoration Types

  • Smooth: Plain, unmarked surface
  • Zonate: Colored bands or rings along the stipe
  • Scaly: Raised bumps or scale-like markings
  • Fibrous/Floccose: Hairy, woolly, or thread-like appearance
  • Grooved: Vertical lines or grooves

What to Look For

Examine the stipe carefully for any patterns, zones, or texture changes. Note separately the appearance near the annulus and below it.

Amanita centriobscura — specimen 30215

Amanita multisquamosa — specimen 30223

Amanita sinicoflava — specimen 30198

Staining

Definition

Color changes or discoloration of the stipe from bruising, handling, or oxidation.

Staining Types

  • No staining: Uniform color with no discoloration
  • Brown staining: Bruised areas turn brown
  • Yellow staining: Oxidation produces yellowing
  • Red/Pink staining: Some species stain reddish when bruised

What to Look For

Observe if there are any discolored areas from handling or weather. Note whether staining occurs quickly or slowly after bruising.

Amanita amerirubescens nom prov. — specimen 30264

Amanita brunnescens — specimen 30272

Annulus (Ring)

Definition

A ring or collar of tissue on the stipe, remnant of the partial veil. Its presence, position, and appearance are diagnostic.

Annulus Conditions

  • Present: Ring is visible on the stipe
  • Absent: No ring structure
  • Superior: Ring located in upper third of stipe
  • Median: Ring located in middle of stipe
  • Inferior: Ring located in lower third of stipe

What to Look For

Look for a distinct ring or collar on the stipe. Note its position and whether the underside is grooved or smooth. In young mushrooms, the annulus may be attached to the cap edge.

Amanita suballiacea — specimen 30187

Amanita chrysoblema — specimen 30210

Amanita frostiana — specimen 30242

Universal Veil Remnant (Base)

Definition

Veil material remaining on the lower stipe and base, forming the volva or scattered fragments.

Veil Remnants

  • Saccate volva: Cup-like pouch enclosing the stipe base
  • Free volva: Bag-like structure loosely surrounding the base
  • Fragmentary: Scattered pieces of veil at the base
  • Absent: No veil remnants visible

What to Look For

Carefully dig around the base to expose any veil material. Pull the mushroom straight up to avoid damaging the volva. Check if the veil is firmly attached or loose.

Amanita fusca — specimen 30186

Amanita centriobscura — specimen 30220

Amanita multisquamosa — specimen 30223

Stipe Context / Flesh Color

Definition

The color of the stipe flesh when cut open, just below the surface.

Context Colors

  • White: Most common
  • Pale yellow: Slightly yellowish
  • Yellow: Distinctly yellow
  • Pink/Red: Reddish or pinkish tissue

What to Look For

Cut the stipe lengthwise to observe the internal flesh color. Note if it differs from the surface color or changes upon exposure to air.

Photo: Stipe Cross-Section Examples

Volva (Base Cup)

The volva is the cup-like or sac-like structure at the base of the stipe, formed from the universal veil. It is one of the most diagnostic features for Amanita identification.

Form

Definition

The overall shape and structure of the volva at the base of the mushroom.

Volva Forms

  • Saccate: Deep, cup-like pouch enclosing the stipe base
  • Free: Loose, bag-like structure not adhered to the stipe
  • Rim: Simple collar or rim around the stipe base
  • Patchy: Broken, irregular pieces around the base
  • Absent: No volva present

What to Look For

Gently dig around the base to expose the volva. A spoon is an incredibly effective Amanita extractor.Pull up the mushroom carefully to see if there is a cup, rings, or just scattered debris. Different forms are diagnostic of different species.

Amanita fusca — specimen 30281

Amanita centriobscura — specimen 30278

Amanita frostiana — specimen 30190

Color

Definition

The color of the volva tissue, which often differs from the cap or stipe color.

Common Volva Colors

  • White: Pure or off-white
  • Pale/Cream: Light yellow or pale tan
  • Yellow/Brown: Brownish or earthy tones
  • Gray: Silvery or muted gray

What to Look For

Observe the volva color in natural light. The color can vary depending on moisture and soil conditions. Compare it to the stipe color for contrast.

Amanita fusca — specimen 30281

Amanita multisquamosa — specimen 30223

Amanita frostiana — specimen 30191

Texture

Definition

The surface texture of the volva: whether it is smooth, scaly, fibrous, or membranous.

Texture Types

  • Smooth: Plain, even surface
  • Scaly: Raised bumps or scales on surface
  • Fibrous: Thread-like or hair-like texture
  • Membranous: Thin, papery, delicate appearance

What to Look For

Feel the volva gently and observe the surface under magnification if possible. The texture can help distinguish between similar-looking volvae.

Amanita fusca — specimen 30281

Amanita cf. oberwinkleriana — specimen 30183

Amanita brunnescens — specimen 30272

Attachment

Definition

How the volva is connected to or positioned relative to the stipe base (firmly attached, loose, or free).

Attachment Types

  • Firmly attached: Volva is adhered to the stipe
  • Loose: Volva is not firmly attached; may move
  • Free: Volva is a separate bag-like structure

What to Look For

Gently try to move the volva relative to the stipe. Note whether it is a cup that moves freely or a structure that appears fused to the stem base.

Amanita multisquamosa — specimen 30223

Amanita cf. oberwinkleriana — specimen 30183

Distribution / Coverage

Definition

How the volva is distributed around the stipe base: complete enclosure, partial coverage, or scattered pieces.

Distribution Patterns

  • Complete: Volva encircles the entire stipe base
  • Incomplete/Patchy: Volva covers only part of the base
  • Scattered: Veil fragments are dispersed around the base

What to Look For

Examine the stipe base from all angles. Note if the volva completely surrounds the stipe or only portions of it. Some volvae appear as a complete rim; others as scattered patches.

Tree host and habitat information

Amanita species form mycorrhizal relationships with specific tree species. They are usually generalists that can associate with Oak or Conifers; although some species have been found associated with Birch, Beech, Maple, and Poplar.

Identifying the Host Tree

What to Look For

Examine the trees immediately surrounding the mushroom. Look for a tree within 1-3 meters (3-10 feet) of the mushroom. Note multiple tree species if present.

Scientific Tree Names

Definition

The binomial scientific names for tree species commonly associated with Wisconsin Amanita species.

Common Wisconsin Trees (Scientific Names)

  • Oak: Quercus species (Q. alba, Q. rubra, Q. macrocarpa)
  • Birch: Betula species (B. papyrifera, B. pubescens)
  • Pine: Pinus species (P. strobus, P. banksiana)
  • Spruce: Picea species (P. mariana, P. glauca)
  • Fir: Abies species (A. balsamea)
  • Hemlock: Tsuga canadensis
  • Larch: Larix species

What to Look For

Record the common and scientific names of trees near your specimen. This information is valuable for future reference and database records. A field guide or tree identification app can assist in confirmation.

Habitat Preferences

Definition

Environmental conditions and forest types where Amanita species are typically found in relation to their tree hosts.

Common Habitat Types

  • Deciduous forest: Oak-dominated or mixed hardwood stands
  • Mixed forest: Transition zones with both deciduous and conifer trees
  • Coniferous forest: Pine, spruce, and fir dominated areas
  • Forest edge: Margins between different forest types
  • Disturbed areas: Roadsides, recently cleared areas, or parks with planted trees

A note on soil type

Soil make up plays a fundamental role in the moisture retention and nutrient availability in a habitat which ultimately influences fungal species composition. If possible, note the soil type:

  • Sandy: Coarse, fast-draining, low nutrient and moisture retention
  • Loamy: Balanced mix of sand, silt, and clay; good drainage and fertility
  • Clay: Fine-particled, dense, and slow-draining; retains moisture
  • Silty: Smooth, fertile, and moisture-retentive but can compact easily
  • Peaty/Organic: High organic matter, acidic, found in bogs and wet lowlands
  • Rocky/Gravelly: Shallow, well-drained soil with abundant stone content

What to Look For

Note the overall forest composition and habitat type. Amanita fruiting is influenced by moisture, temperature, and tree community. The combination of habitat and tree host can help narrow species identification.

Using This Guide

Now that you understand the key morphological features of Amanita mushrooms, you can use this knowledge in several ways:

  • Field Observations: Take careful notes of these features when you encounter Amanita species in the field.
  • Photography: Capture photos of the cap, volva, stipe, and base from multiple angles to support your identification.
  • Search Tool: Use our Search tool to query the database with the morphological features you observe.
  • Dichotomous Key: Follow the Dichotomous Key to narrow down species identifications.
